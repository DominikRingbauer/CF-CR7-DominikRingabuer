More luxury

In the past, luxury was associated with sports cars and casinos. Apparently that has changed. At ITB Berlin, the world's largest tourism fair, the term was redefined: “Luxury as we understand it today has nothing to do with the bling bling of 20 years ago,” explained Marc Aeberhard, owner of a luxury hotel group. Today it is more about immaterial factors, about the gain of space and time, about individual control. About personal service. About security. And less stress so that you can concentrate on the essentials. About untouched nature and the experience of solitude. And yourself. At the ITB, a luxury tour operator focused on untouched travel destinations in the wilderness of the north. The coordinates are not revealed, smartphones and social media activities are prohibited. Sustainability is also important: those who stay longer pay less.

More tourists

More and more people are traveling. In 2018, the number of trips abroad rose by 5.5 percent, which corresponds to a total of 1.4 billion trips abroad. The vast majority of these are pleasure trips, according to the current World Travel Monitor from IPK International. The growth in tourism comes from all regions of the world, including the traditionally strong markets of Europe and North America. Among the target regions, Asia and also Europe were the winners on the world map, each with 6 percent growth. From a global perspective, both the average duration of travel and travel expenses increased in 2018, with the result that international tourism achieved global sales growth of eight percent in 2018. In general, vacation trips developed better than business trips in 2018. While MICE travel continues to grow, traditional business travel is still on a downward trend.
